## **What is reinforcement learning?** 

- People and animals learn by **interacting with our environment** 

   - This differs from certain other types of learning 

   - It is **active** rather than passive 

- Interactions are often **sequential** — future interactions can depend on earlier ones 

- We are **goal-directed** 

- We can learn **without examples** of optimal behaviour 

- Instead, we optimise some **reward signal**

## ~~ee~~ **Curiosity** ~~**killed**~~ **saved the cat** 

Modern RL suffers from two problems: 

- the **sparse rewards or non-existing rewards problem** : in many problems, most intermediate rewards do not contain information and hence are set to zero. 

- the **extrinsic reward function is hand-engineered:** it may be suboptimal or lead to unintended behaviours 

- The idea of curiosity-driven learning is to build a reward function that is **intrinsic** to the agent (that is generated by the agent itself, rather than by external environment). In this sense, the agent will act as a self-learner since it will be the student, but also provide its own feedback. 

## **Curiosity as a form of exploration** 

Curiosity can be implemented as predicting the next state: the reward is the prediction error. 

The agent experiences spikes of intrinsic reward when it sees a new configuration of bricks early on in training and when it passes the level for the first time after training for several hours. 

https://openai.com/index/reinforcement-learning-with-prediction-based-rewards/ 

## **Curiosity** ~~**saved**~~ **killed the cat** 

The “Noisy-TV” problem started as a thought experiment in Burda, et al (2018). If an RL agent is rewarded with seeking novel experience, a TV with unpredictable random noise (→ high prediction error → high reward) would be able to attract the agent’s attention forever. 

(Image source: OpenAI Blog: "Reinforcement Learning with Prediction-Based Rewards")
